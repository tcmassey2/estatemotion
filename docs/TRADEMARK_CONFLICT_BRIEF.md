## 3. The questions for counsel

1. **Clearance:** Given the senior common-law user above in the same broad field (real
   estate listing video), how strong is a likelihood-of-confusion problem? Does it block
   registration, or only limit applicant's rights in the senior user's geographic zone?
2. **Distinguishing factors — how much do these help?**
   - Applicant is **SaaS / self-serve software** (Class 9/42); senior user is a **local,
     done-for-you photography/video service** (different trade channel, different buying
     process, arguably different class).
   - Senior user appears **geographically limited** to Chicagoland; applicant is online/nationwide.
   - Marks differ visually by spacing ("EstateMotion" vs "Estate Motion") — assume this is
     legally weak but note it.
3. **Filing strategy:** 1(a) use-based vs **1(b) intent-to-use** (applicant is pre-launch).
   How should the sworn declaration handle applicant's **actual knowledge** of the senior
   user? Any concentual-coexistence or consent-agreement worth pursuing proactively?
4. **Risk of waking the senior user:** does filing/publication realistically invite an
   opposition or cease-and-desist, and how should we weigh that vs. filing early to secure
   a constructive-use date?
5. **Defensive posture:** what's the cheapest registration that meaningfully protects the
   SaaS product, and should we pair the word mark with a stylized/logo mark?

## 4. Applicant's stated business goal

Proceed under the EstateMotion name, coexisting with the senior user (different segment,
different channel, different customer), and obtain whatever federal protection is cleanly
available for the **SaaS product** — without picking a fight with, or being ambushed by,
the senior user. Decision rule: if counsel finds material risk, applicant will consider
differentiating the mark (distinctive logo/tagline) before considering a rebrand.
